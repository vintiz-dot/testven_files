Annotation

A single integrated annotation covering the five stages of the design cycle as presented in the video, and how each stage connects to classroom learning and teacher planning.

The Cycle, As Taught

1. EmpathizeInterview the user. Listen for emotion.
2. DefineState the unmet need + the insight.
3. IdeateSketch 5+ ideas. Quantity over quality.
4. PrototypeBuild something tangible — rough is the point.
5. TestPut it in the user's hands. Listen, do not sell.

What the Video Actually Teaches

The d.school crash course is a live, time-boxed workshop run by two facilitators who hand each pair a single prompt — redesign your partner's gift-giving experience — and then immediately drop them into the work. There is no theory lecture; the methodology is delivered through the experience of doing it under a clock. In roughly 80 minutes the pairs cycle through all five stages of design thinking and arrive at a tested physical prototype, however imperfect. The facilitators repeatedly model four mindsets they treat as more important than the cycle itself: be human-centered (build for a specific person, not a market); be iterative (treat every artifact as a probe, not a final answer); be collaborative (your partner is your co-investigator, not your audience); and have a bias toward prototyping and action (do, then reflect — not the other way around).

Stage by stage, here is what the workshop demonstrates and what each stage offers a teacher or instructional designer in their own planning:

Stage What the video does How it lands in a classroom / teacher planning
Empathize Two 4-minute interviews. The second interview is explicitly told to "go beyond the tactical" and chase emotion — stories, motivations, discomfort. This is the move teachers most often skip when redesigning a unit. The first cycle of student interviews captures procedural pain ("the directions are confusing"); the second cycle reveals motivational pain ("I don't try because I don't think I'm a math person"). A unit designed only from the first cycle solves the wrong problem.
Define Participants synthesize their interview into a list of verb-based needs and emotional insights, then write one short, specific problem statement. The facilitator's rubric: "short, specific, sexy." This is the analog of writing a learning objective from the student's perspective rather than the curriculum's. Instead of "Students will solve two-step equations," the framing becomes "Hana, a confident reader who avoids math because she equates speed with intelligence, needs a way to slow down without feeling behind." That problem statement drives different instructional choices than the curriculum-first version.
Ideate Five minutes, five sketches, no letters or numbers allowed. The facilitator celebrates the participant who produced 37 sketches, not the one who produced the most polished one. For teacher planning, this is permission to brainstorm before filtering. Most lesson planning collapses ideate and evaluate into the same step, which kills divergence. Treating ideation as a separate, timed, low-stakes activity (even alone, on sticky notes) consistently produces better lessons than going straight to the "what will we do Monday" question.
Prototype Ten minutes with paper, foil, tape, and popsicle sticks. The deliberate choice of crummy materials lowers ego — nobody mistakes the artifact for a finished product. In a classroom this is a paper draft of a station rotation, a hand-drawn rubric, a 90-second voice memo explaining a lesson before slides exist. The instructional equivalent of "build it ugly fast" is what makes peer feedback honest. Polished plans invite politeness; rough ones invite real reactions.
Test Four minutes per partner with a feedback grid: what worked / what failed / questions / new ideas. Facilitators tell participants to "be an anthropologist, not a salesman." The hardest skill to teach new teachers is to watch a lesson fail in real time without rescuing it. The anthropologist stance — observe, note, do not defend — is exactly the stance a teacher needs during a student work session and during instructional rounds. The feedback grid is reusable verbatim as a lesson-debrief tool.

The Mindsets the Cycle Hangs Off

Human-CenteredThe user is one specific person at your table, not a generalized "student." Empathy is the source of the design, not a courtesy.
IterativeLinear is the teaching version of the cycle. The working version is non-linear — you can prototype before defining and re-empathize after testing.
CollaborativeInnovation is a team sport. The video deliberately puts strangers together and forbids working alone.
Bias Toward ActionIf you are talking a lot, that is a signal to get up and try something. "Do then reflect," not "think then do."

Where This Connects to Teacher Planning

The video's most useful contribution to teacher planning specifically is that it reframes lesson design as a research-and-prototype loop instead of an authorship task. A teacher who has been through this workshop is more likely to: (a) interview two or three students before redesigning a unit; (b) write the learning need from the student's perspective, not from the standard; (c) draft three rough versions of a lesson before picking one; (d) ship the lesson knowing it is a prototype and that feedback comes from the kids, not from the lesson plan itself. None of this requires new technology. It requires a small shift in where teachers start when they plan.

For an instructional designer or coach, the value is even more direct: the d.school cycle is essentially a compressed, vocabulary-rich version of ADDIE's Analyze and Evaluate phases, and it can be dropped into PD as a one-hour activity that gives teachers a shared vocabulary for the practice of noticing. That shared vocabulary — "empathy interview," "insight," "POV statement," "rough prototype," "anthropologist" — outlasts the workshop itself and becomes useful in the next planning meeting.

Final Annotation: Reflection

I enjoyed this video because the material is very familiar to me. I spent most of my career doing this kind of work — running design workshops, building interactive prototypes, and running usability sessions with real users in markets around the world. Watching the d.school crash course felt like watching a clean, well-edited version of a day I have lived dozens of times.

What the video does well, and what I think is hard to do well, is teach a method of attention rather than a checklist. The five stages are easy to memorize and almost beside the point. The harder thing the facilitators are trying to install is the willingness to keep your eye on the user instead of on the artifact — to seek learning rather than validation, to be an anthropologist rather than a salesman. Those are stance changes, not skill changes, and they are slow.

"Seek learning, not validation. Take on the attitude of a learner. These sketches are an artifact to facilitate a deeper understanding of your user."

They also do, in my view, a useful over-correction. Most working adults — including most teachers — have an enormous bias toward feasibility, polish, and being right the first time. The crash course leans hard the other way: crummy materials, time pressure, no alphanumerics, celebrate quantity. As pedagogy that is correct. You cannot teach someone to prototype while also asking them to do it well. The over-correction is the lesson.

I do, however, want to flag where this method is incomplete, because I have seen it fail. Years ago I ran a workshop very much like this one with a group of senior German pharmaceutical executives. They could not step outside of their pre-conceived notions about who their audience was or what insight was “allowed” to count. It was an uncomfortable session and a clarifying one: not everyone is ready to be a design thinker, and a one-hour experience cannot make you one. When your audience self-selects — the people who showed up to the d.school — you can skip that conversation. When you are running this for your own school's teachers, you cannot. The people who most need design thinking are usually the people least eager to sign up for it, and your workshop has to account for that resistance up front, not pretend it is not in the room.

Finally, the line from the video that I keep with me is “bias toward action.” I had it printed and pinned over my desk as a designer; it is still on the wall now that I am an educator. The work I want to do as an instructional designer and coach is to help my school move out of the think tank — abstract, isolated from kids, organized around opinion — and into a do tank, where experimentation and reflection share the same week. The d.school video is a clean, honest, 80-minute argument for that move. I expect to use chunks of it in the design thinking workshop I am brainstorming for Greenfield Prep, especially the test-with-a-rough-prototype segment, which is the part teachers most often skip and the part that pays back the fastest.
