Designing & Assessing Real-World Math

January 2026 InTASC 4, 5, 6 Victor Moronu

1. Summary of Implementation

In this unit, I implemented an authentic performance assessment challenging Grade 7 students to apply factoring and the distributive property (7.EE.A.1) to real-world design scenarios. Students chose between designing "Supply Kits" for a community orphanage (interpreting GCF as the number of kits) or planning a "Mural Layout" (interpreting factors as dimensions).

Successes & Live Evidence

The transition from procedural fluency to conceptual application was stark. The "Maximization vs. Value" debate that spontaneously erupted during the Supply Kit activity was a highlight (InTASC 5). Students argued whether it was better to have more small kits (High GCF) or fewer substantial kits (Lower Common Factor). This proved they weren't just calculating numbers; they were analyzing the implications of those numbers.

Watch the Live Assessment: Observe students engaging in the "Mural Layout" reasoning and board work.

Challenges

While engagement was high, the debate occasionally drifted into "philosophy" rather than mathematics. One student noted, "This is becoming philosophy, not math," highlighting a need to anchor their arguments more firmly in quantitative data. Additionally, some students struggled with the initial leap from abstract variables ($12x + 18y$) to concrete items, requiring significant scaffolding with visual bar models.

2. Guiding Self- & Peer-Assessment

To foster ownership (InTASC 6), I introduced the analytic rubric before the final product creation. We used a "Simplified Checklist" version for peer review:

  • Self-Assessment: Students assessed their initial drafts against the "Mathematical Accuracy" criterion. I asked them to highlight where they showed their GCF calculation explicitly.
  • Peer-Feedback Loop: In pairs, students exchanged planning sheets. I provided sentence stems ("I see you factored... but have you considered...") to structure their feedback. This was crucial for my ELL students (Student 3) to participate meaningfully.

3. Analysis of Student Achievement

Data from the final rubric scoring revealed distinct patterns:

  • Advanced Learners (Student 1): Excelled in "Critical Thinking." Student 1 didn't just find the GCF; he diagrammed the mural dimensions to prove why certain factor pairs were spatially impossible, demonstrating deep mastery of geometric application.
  • ELL Students (Student 3): Showed significant growth in "Mathematical Accuracy" (90%) but struggled with the "Explanation" criterion due to linguistic load. However, the visual nature of the mural task allowed Student 3 to demonstrate understanding without relying solely on complex text.
  • Students with Learning Needs (Student 2): Struggled with "Organization." While the math was often correct, the connection between their factored expression and their design decision was sometimes lost. This indicates a need for more graphic organizers in the planning phase.

4. Evaluating the Rubric

What Worked

The "Critical Thinking" row was essential. It allowed me to reward students who proposed creative, less obvious factorizations (e.g., opting for 6 larger kits instead of 12 small ones) even if it wasn't the "standard" textbook answer.

Future Revisions

I need to refine the "Communication" criterion. It currently conflates "presentation skills" with "mathematical explanation." In the next iteration, I will split this to explicitly assess "Use of Mathematical Vocabulary" separately from "Presentation Clarity" to be fairer to ELLs.

5. Plan for Improvement

Based on this analysis, I will implement two specific changes for the next iteration:

  1. The "Data-Based Rebuttal" Protocol: To prevent the "philosophy drift" observed in the debate, I will require students to calculate the exact unit rate per person for each proposed scenario before they are allowed to argue for it. This forces the critical thinking back to the evidence.
  2. "Think-Pair-Share" for Board Work: I observed some performance anxiety when students presented their initial factorizations. I will implement a structured 30-second peer consultation period before any student is asked to present their solution publicly, lowering the affective filter.
