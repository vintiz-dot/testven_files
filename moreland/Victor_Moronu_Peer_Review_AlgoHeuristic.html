Opening Note

Thank you for sharing this submission. Choosing Algo-Heuristic Theory is a bold call — it is one of the less commonly adopted models in the cohort, and the decision to pair it with a resource-constrained problem (“not enough devices”) shows real intentionality about fit between problem and framework. Below are my genuine takeaways after a careful read, followed by concrete, actionable suggestions. My own submission used ADDIE, so my comments are intentionally shaped by that contrast.

What’s Working Well

1. Strong problem-to-model fit. The central logic of Algo-Heuristic — pair a reliable algorithm (the step-by-step) with a flexible heuristic (the mental shortcut) — is a genuinely elegant match for a low-device environment, where teachers cannot rely on any single tech-driven procedure and must adapt on the fly. This pairing is the single strongest intellectual move in the submission.

2. Concrete classroom examples. The two real-world examples — Elementary Math multi-step word problems and Secondary ELA theme analysis — each include an explicit student algorithm (Read → Solve → Check; Read → Identify theme → Find evidence → Explain) paired with a well-chosen heuristic question (“Is there a better way to solve this?” / “Is the main message applicable to real life?”). This is exactly the level of specificity a coach needs to show teachers — not just the model, but how it moves into a student’s hands.

3. Scalable, implementable tactics. Rotating stations, 1-device-per-group, peer teaching, and peer assessment are all low-lift interventions that teachers can actually adopt. These are not aspirational — they are implementable next Monday.

4. Cross-framework thinking. The mention of the OSKAR coaching model alongside Algo-Heuristic suggests you are already thinking in layers: Algo-Heuristic is your design model, OSKAR is your coaching frame. That is mature design thinking and worth making more visible in the narrative.

5. Honest self-assessment of the model’s popularity. You correctly flag that Algo-Heuristic is less mainstream than ADDIE or UbD and that this carries a research/legibility cost. That self-awareness strengthens the argument rather than weakens it.

Ideas for Improvement

1. Strengthen the Evaluation layer.

Algo-Heuristic is a design-and-practice framework; it does not have a built-in evaluation phase the way ADDIE does. This is the single biggest gap I noticed. Consider overlaying Kirkpatrick’s Four Levels on top of your five Algo-Heuristic steps so the workshop has a measurable impact story:

Level 1 (Reaction): exit ticket after each session.

Level 2 (Learning): a pre/post “lesson redesign” task — teachers translate a heavy-tech lesson into a low-tech version and are scored on an observable rubric.

Level 3 (Behavior): classroom walkthroughs over the following month, logging whether rotating stations, peer teaching, or 1-device-per-group is visibly in use.

Level 4 (Results): student achievement on low-tech tasks vs. prior tech-dependent tasks; attendance/engagement during low-tech lessons.

Without this, it is hard to show admin whether the workshop moved the needle on the “not enough devices” pain point.

2. Specify the teacher audience.

Right now it is unclear whether the workshop is pitched at elementary teachers, secondary teachers, or the whole faculty. The two student-level examples span both ends of the K–12 range, but the workshop itself needs a target audience so that the algorithms you teach them are grade-appropriate. A quick fix: add an “Audience” row at the top of the template (e.g., “12 Grade 3–5 teachers” or “20 secondary ELA/Math teachers”).

3. Add a concrete timeline and coaching arc.

The template references “as the weeks go by” and “a follow-up workshop,” but the workshop’s duration, number of sessions, and 1:1 coaching cadence are not specified. A suggested structure:

Session 1 (90 min): algorithm building — teachers co-author the shared algorithm ‘Plan → Teach → Assess → Reflect.’

Session 2 (90 min): heuristic development — teachers co-author 3–5 reusable heuristics and run a low-tech lesson redesign.

Weeks 3–6: 1:1 coaching — each teacher implements one low-tech lesson; coach observes and debriefs.

Week 7: follow-up workshop — share-out, data review, and adjustment.

4. Expand the heuristics with a named library.

The heuristics in the document are mostly open-ended questions (“What’s the easiest way to meet the objective?”). These are strong as starting prompts, but teachers benefit from a named, reusable set. Consider defining 4–6 heuristics by name so they travel across lessons — for example:

The “Print, Don’t Scan” heuristic — if the material lives in a PDF, it can live on paper.

The “1-Device-Per-Group” heuristic — design the activity so the device is the station, not the student.

The “Peer-as-Processor” heuristic — when a tool would answer a question, a peer can sometimes answer faster.

The “Lowest-Tech-That-Works” heuristic — start from paper and only add technology when it provably adds value.

Naming them turns a mental shortcut into a piece of culture teachers can reference by shorthand.

5. Materials list and shared artifact folder.

Add a simple materials list so the workshop is reproducible: printed lesson-plan templates, whiteboards, station task cards, a shared Google Drive folder with low-tech lesson exemplars, and a teacher-facing “low-tech redesign checklist.” Without it, the workshop lives in the coach’s head.

6. Briefly explain OSKAR.

The OSKAR reference is a nice connection, but a reader unfamiliar with it may miss the move. One sentence — “OSKAR (Outcome → Scaling → Know-how → Affirm & Action → Review) is the coaching-conversation frame I use on top of the design model” — would let the reviewer see the two-layer architecture.

7. Deepen the model comparison.

The narrative compares Algo-Heuristic to ADDIE and UbD. Two additional comparisons worth a sentence each:

Merrill’s First Principles: Merrill’s task-centered principle overlaps with Algo-Heuristic’s Step 1 (Task Analysis). Worth flagging the overlap — the two models are more compatible than competing.

Dick & Carey: another systems-approach model that could have worked here, but like ADDIE is heavier on analysis than Algo-Heuristic, which would have slowed a resource-constrained school down.

8. Small proofreading.

Step #2: “cohearant” should be “coherent.”

Step #5: “LP’s” is possessive; for plural lesson plans use “LPs” (no apostrophe).

Narrative: “outlook overtime” should be “over time” (two words).

Questions to Deepen the Thinking

If admin asks “how will we know this worked?” six weeks from now, what data do you point to? That answer probably belongs at the top of Step #5 Application.

How do you want to handle the teacher who tries the low-tech redesign once and returns to a device-heavy default? That’s where coaching (OSKAR) does the work; is it worth making that explicit as a sixth step?

Are there subjects where Algo-Heuristic fits less naturally — e.g., a digital media or computer science class where the device is the content? Naming the limit would strengthen the model’s credibility.

What I Took Away from Your Submission

The strongest contribution of this submission — beyond the template itself — is the decision to match an uncommon model to an uncommon constraint. Most instructional coaching workshops assume abundant technology and ask “how do we leverage it?”; your submission asks “how do we coach teachers to thrive without it?” That is a more honest question for a lot of schools, and Algo-Heuristic is arguably a better lens for it than ADDIE would have been. In my own ADDIE-based submission, I focused on reducing device distractions in a 1:1 environment — effectively the opposite problem. Reading your submission sharpened my thinking about the assumption of device abundance that ADDIE often carries.

Summary Verdict

Substantively proficient with Outstanding-level potential after strengthening the Evaluation layer and adding an audience/timeline specification. The model choice is defensible and the classroom-level examples are genuinely useful to any cohort member facing device scarcity.
