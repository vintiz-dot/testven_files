Mod 40 Unit 1 LMS Analysis Completed

Learning Management Systems Investigation

A collaborative comparative analysis of four prominent Learning Management Systems, evaluating communication options, assessment tools, and strategic adoption procedures.

Academic Overview & Rationale

Overview: As classrooms transition towards blended and 1:1 models, the selection of a Learning Management System (LMS) is foundational. An effective LMS must not only manage curriculum and assessment but also streamline teacher-student-parent communication and align deeply with the school’s overarching technology vision.

Rationale: This project evaluates four diverse LMS platforms (Google Classroom, Canvas, Schoology, and Moodle). The goal was to look past the marketing features and critically analyze how content is shared, what analytical tools are provided for formative assessment, and what the logistical process of proposing adoption to district leadership would entail.

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

What happened?

I collaborated with a partner to research and build a presentation evaluating four major Learning Management Systems. We synthesized data regarding their origins, content-sharing capabilities, assessment tools, and communication structures, culminating in a structured adoption proposal.

What were you thinking and feeling?

I initially felt overwhelmed by the sheer volume of features each platform boasted. However, organizing the analysis into standardized categories (Assessment, Communication, etc.) on Padlet made the comparative process feel much more manageable and visually clear.

What was good and bad?

The collaborative aspect was highly effective—splitting the research allowed for deeper dives into each platform. A downside was trying to ascertain the true "usability" of some platforms without having full administrative sandbox access to test backend workflows.

What sense can you make of it?

A key realization is that there is no "perfect" LMS; rather, there is only the LMS that best fits a school's specific hardware ecosystem (e.g., 1:1 Chromebooks vs. BYOD) and faculty technical literacy. Buy-in and training are often more critical than the software itself.

What else could you have done?

If expanding this research, I would have surveyed current faculty members across different departments to gather qualitative data on the specific pain points they currently experience with our existing setup.

What will you do next time?

When tasked with proposing new technology in the future, I will always start by auditing the current infrastructure and faculty needs first, using the outline developed in this assignment to frame the cost-benefit analysis for administration.

Mod 40 Integration Analysis Completed

Technology Integration Models

A comprehensive, interactive analysis of SAMR, TPACK, and PICRAT models to determine the optimal framework for localized pedagogical transformation.

Context & Academic Summary

Overview: When bringing technology into the classroom, it is critical to provide educators with a clear model to visualize the systemic changes taking place. This project evaluates prevalent models, including SAMR, TPACK, and PICRAT, exploring their strengths, practical applications, and critiques.

Rationale: The fundamental goal of integration is to move beyond mere substitution. By establishing a shared vocabulary via the PICRAT framework, staff at a bilingual campus without a centralized LMS can systematically shift student engagement from passive consumption to active, language-rich digital creation.

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

1. Description (What happened?)

What happened?

I researched and synthesized three major technology integration frameworks (SAMR, TPACK, and PICRAT), ultimately determining which model would be most effective for faculty deployment at my bilingual campus.

What were you thinking and feeling?

I felt intellectually engaged by TPACK's theoretical depth but realized quickly that its complexity might alienate educators already overwhelmed by learning new digital tools.

What was good and bad?

The comparative analysis was excellent — it forced me to see the limitations of SAMR's linear "ladder" approach. The challenge was finding a model that honored teacher effort while prioritizing student cognitive engagement.

What sense can you make of it?

I concluded that PICRAT is the superior model for my context. By isolating the student's relationship to technology (Passive vs. Creative) from the teacher's use of it, PICRAT offers a highly visual matrix for auditing a lesson plan without overwhelming jargon.

What else could you have done?

I could have expanded the analysis to include the adoption lifecycle of these models mapped against our specific campus demographics and tech fluency levels.

What will you do next time?

I will build professional development sessions entirely around the PICRAT matrix, challenging teachers to make the +1 Shift — consciously moving a lesson from Passive-Replacement to Interactive-Amplification.
