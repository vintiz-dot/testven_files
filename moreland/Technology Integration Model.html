Pedagogical Research Synthesis

Evaluating EdTech Integration

An interactive, research-based analysis of SAMR, TPACK, and PICRAT models to determine the optimal framework for localized pedagogical transformation.

Core Concept

SAMR divides integration into two phases: Enhancement (Substitution, Augmentation) where tech acts as a direct tool substitute, and Transformation (Modification, Redefinition) where tech allows for significant task redesign or entirely new tasks.

Practical Application

Teachers use it to self-audit. "Am I just digitizing a worksheet (Substitution), or am I having students collaborate globally on a multimedia project (Redefinition)?" It provides a clear, aspirational trajectory.

Expansive Research & Critique

Academic Depth: Puentedura's model is heavily utilized due to its accessible vernacular. However, academic critiques (e.g., Hamilton et al., 2016) note that SAMR lacks a focus on context and pedagogy. It implicitly suggests that "Redefinition" is always superior, whereas sometimes "Substitution" is the most pedagogically appropriate choice for a specific learning objective.

Hanoi Context Application: In a developing infrastructure, pushing for Redefinition can cause frustration. SAMR must be used carefully here, ensuring teachers do not feel inadequate if they remain in the Enhancement phase while building baseline digital fluency.

TPACK

Mishra & Koehler (2006)

A highly theoretical framework identifying the complex interplay of knowledge required by teachers to effectively integrate technology into their teaching.

Core Concept

Builds on Shulman’s (1986) PCK (Pedagogical Content Knowledge). It asserts that effective teaching requires an understanding of how Technology Knowledge (TK), Pedagogy (PK), and Content (CK) interact dynamically.

Practical Application

Used heavily in teacher-prep programs. Before adopting a tool, teachers ask: "Does this technology (TK) actually support the specific pedagogy (PK) I need to teach this specific subject matter (CK)?"

Expansive Research & Intersections

Academic Depth: The power of TPACK lies in its intersections. TCK (Technological Content Knowledge) involves knowing how subject matter is changed by tech (e.g., teaching anatomy via 3D VR vs. textbooks). TPK (Technological Pedagogical Knowledge) involves understanding how teaching changes with tech (e.g., using forums for asynchronous discussion). True integration, the center, recognizes teaching with tech as a "Wicked Problem" requiring fluid, context-specific solutions rather than a one-size-fits-all approach.

Critique: While intellectually robust, researchers note boundaries between constructs (e.g., TPK vs. PCK) are often blurry for practicing educators, making it difficult to use as a quick, daily evaluative tool.

PICRAT

Kimmons, Graham, & West (2020)

A modern, highly actionable two-dimensional matrix evaluating both what the student is doing and how the teacher is utilizing the technology.

Core Concept

Cross-references the Student's Relationship to technology (Passive, Interactive, Creative) against the Teacher's Practice (Replacement, Amplification, Transformation). It results in 9 distinct integration coordinates.

Practical Application

Teachers plot a specific activity on the matrix. The institutional goal is upward and rightward mobility—shifting students from passive consumers to creative producers (the "CT" quadrant).

Expansive Research & Matrix Detail

Academic Depth: Developed specifically to overcome the shortcomings of SAMR and TPACK. Kimmons et al. recognized that previous models often ignored the student's cognitive state. By isolating student actions (PIC) from teacher actions (RAT), it clarifies integration. A teacher could use highly transformative tech (VR), but if the student is merely passively watching it (PT), the integration falls short of pedagogical excellence.

  • Passive (P): Student consumes information (watching a video).
  • Interactive (I): Student interacts with the medium (completing a digital drag-and-drop).
  • Creative (C): Student generates original artifacts (recording a podcast).
Final Determination

Recommended Model:
PICRAT

Based on our structural analysis and the localized needs of our Hanoi campus, the PICRAT model is the definitively superior framework. It bridges theoretical rigor with immediate, visual practicality.

Empirical Justification

  • Cognitive Focus: It mandates evaluation of student output (PIC), which is scientifically proven to be a stronger indicator of learning than teacher input alone.
  • Actionable Metrics: It removes the ambiguity of TPACK by providing a concrete 3x3 plotting system for lesson planning.

Contextual Alignment: Hanoi Bilingual Campus

Operating without a centralized Learning Management System (LMS) and with diverse staff tech-literacy, our environment demands a low-barrier, grassroots framework.

PICRAT succeeds here because it does not require complex infrastructure. It validates teachers making initial steps (Passive/Replacement using basic tools) while giving our tech-champions a clear vocabulary to drive bilingual students toward higher-order language production (Creative/Transformative output).

Strategic Implementation Phases

Phase 1: Vocabulary

Introduce PICRAT matrix in PD. Establish non-evaluative shared language.

Phase 2: The "+1 Shift"

Challenge staff to move one lesson up or right on the grid (e.g., Passive to Interactive).

Phase 3: Shared Hub

Create a localized Google Drive (bypassing LMS) of PICRAT-mapped bilingual lesson plans.
