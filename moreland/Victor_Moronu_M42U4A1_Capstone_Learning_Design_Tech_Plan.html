Executive Summary

What this is and why now

This document is the Phase II Learning Design Solution and Phase III Technology Implementation Plan for an EdTech Coaching Program at Greenfield Preparatory Middle School. It builds directly on the Phase I information-gathering work (audience analysis, learning gap, goal, Bloom’s levels, ID models reviewed) and on the EdTech & Instructional Coaching Survey conducted in April 2026.

The core insight from Phase I is that Greenfield Prep teachers already operate classroom technology — 13 of 18 use Chromebooks daily — but cannot reliably design with it. Of the 18 teachers surveyed, 67% want 1:1 coaching, 67% rate themselves at or below a 3 (out of 5) on differentiation-with-tech confidence, and 100% of administrators name passive student use as their top concern. Buying more software will not close that gap. Coaching teachers through real instructional design cycles will.

The solution is not a tool. It is a coaching program with three layers (1:1 cycles, PLC data routines, quarterly design sprints), powered by a small, fit-for-purpose technology stack and governed by a tight measurement model.

Phase II · A

Solution overview — how the three layers work together

The solution is a one-year, three-layer coaching program. Each layer addresses a different aspect of the Phase I gap, runs on its own cadence, and feeds the others.

LayerWhat it isFrequencyPrimary outcome
1. 1:1 Coaching Cycle Cognitive-Coaching-based Plan → Observe → Reflect → Apply cycle between the coach and one teacher around a teacher-chosen problem. Weekly, ~30 min One specific, applied instructional move per cycle.
2. PLC Data Routine Grade-team meeting structured around tool dashboards (IXL, Desmos, EdPuzzle) and SAMR-banded walkthrough look-fors. Biweekly, 45 min Shared instructional move from data; calibrated look-fors.
3. Design Thinking Sprint Compressed Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test cycle around a single high-leverage instructional problem. Quarterly, 90 min One student-creation lesson shipped & archived in showcase.

Why three layers instead of one

A single layer cannot do all the work. A coaching cycle alone produces individual gains but no schoolwide consistency. A PLC alone produces shared talk but rarely shared practice. A workshop alone produces an event with a half-life of about three days. Combining the three — with the same vocabulary (TPACK, SAMR, Cognitive Coaching, design thinking) running through all of them — produces individual change, team consistency, and schoolwide visibility.

What is innovative about this design

  • It is not bolted onto the calendar. The 1:1 cycle runs in the teacher’s already-existing planning period. PLCs already exist; we are restructuring them, not adding meetings. The sprint runs in a 90-minute window the school already protects for PD. Adoption is high because nothing new has to be cleared.
  • It assumes teachers know things. Veterans pair with newer teachers as co-coaches, not as students. The program creates a thinking partner, not an expert telling teachers what to do. This is a direct application of Cognitive Coaching and Knowles’ andragogy — respect for prior experience as a design principle.
  • Data is the agenda, not a report. Every PLC starts with a dashboard projected on the wall, not a slide deck. Teachers see real student progress before any decision is made. This collapses the gap teachers named in the survey: “I assign IXL but I don’t know what the data is telling me.”
  • The sprint produces an artifact, not a takeaway. Every design thinking sprint ends with a tested rough prototype in a teacher’s hand, not a list of ideas in a Google doc. The artifact has to ship Monday or it does not count.
Phase II · B

The 1:1 coaching cycle — weekly anatomy

The cycle is a four-step loop run weekly with each enrolled teacher. It is built on Costa & Garmston’s Cognitive Coaching maps with two adaptations: (a) the “observe” step is built around a tool dashboard rather than a classroom visit when relevant, and (b) the “apply” step is logged in a shared coaching folder visible to the admin team in real time.

StepDurationWhat happensArtifact
1. Plan 10 min, Mon Coach & teacher pick one student or one lesson move. Coach uses Cognitive Coaching planning map: “What are your goals? What will you look for? What might get in the way?” Plan card (single page, shared folder)
2. Observe 15 min, Tues/Wed Coach observes the lesson move OR coach & teacher review the relevant dashboard together. Coach is in anthropologist mode — notes, no advice. Observation notes (shared folder)
3. Reflect 10 min, Thurs Cognitive Coaching reflecting map: “What happened? What do you make of it? What will you do differently?” Coach asks; teacher decides. Reflection note (3 lines)
4. Apply during instruction Teacher implements the next move. Coach is available but not present. The change is theirs. Implementation evidence (lesson artifact, photo, student work)

Quarterly focus areas

Each cycle is teacher-chosen, but the program suggests a quarterly emphasis so that the schoolwide pattern is coherent:

  • Q1 (Cycles 1–3): Routines. Device handout, login, focus check, transitions, packup. The “named routine everyone can see” the principal asked for.
  • Q2 (Cycles 4–6): Data moves. Reading an IXL/Desmos/EdPuzzle dashboard and acting on one piece of evidence per week.
  • Q3 (Cycles 7–9): Differentiation. Specific moves for ML and IEP students using existing tools (Google Translate & Newsela for ML; text-to-speech & leveled practice for IEP).
  • Q4 (Cycles 10–12): Creation. Designing a student-creation lesson per teacher; preparing for next year.
Phase II · C

PLC data routine — the biweekly rhythm

PLCs already meet at Greenfield Prep, biweekly, by grade-level team. The program restructures them around a 45-minute protocol that always starts with data on the wall.

BlockDurationActivity
Dashboard up5 minOne dashboard is projected. No discussion yet — just notice.
What do we see?10 minEach teacher names one thing they see in the data. Coach captures patterns on a whiteboard.
What does it mean?10 minTeam analyzes patterns — identifies 2-3 likely instructional implications.
What will we do?15 minEach teacher commits to one specific instructional move for the next two weeks. Logged in shared doc.
Calibration5 minQuick SAMR look-fors calibration using a video clip or a real lesson described.

Why this changes practice

Teachers cited “I don’t know what the data is telling me” as a top frustration. The protocol fixes that by making the dashboard the agenda — not a slide about the dashboard, but the dashboard itself, projected, read together. After three or four PLCs, teachers start reading dashboards before walking into the meeting because they know they will be asked.

Phase II · D

Quarterly design thinking sprint

Once per quarter, the whole faculty runs a 90-minute design thinking sprint built on the d.school cycle (annotated in M42U3A1). The sprint is single-prompted: one shared instructional problem, one cycle, one rough prototype per teacher, one Monday-ready commitment.

The four sprint prompts (one per quarter)

  1. Q1 — “Redesign the First Five Minutes of Chromebook Time.” Targets the routines work and the distraction challenge.
  2. Q2 — “Redesign How You Group Students Using Data.” Targets the data-driven instruction goal.
  3. Q3 — “Redesign One Lesson So Students Create Instead of Consume.” Targets the admin one-year vision directly.
  4. Q4 — Showcase. Each teacher presents one creation lesson from the year. The showcase doubles as the program’s public evidence base.

Sprint protocol (90 min)

  • Empathize (15 min) — interview a partner about one specific student.
  • Define (10 min) — write a one-line POV statement: [student] needs [verb] because [emotional insight].
  • Ideate (15 min) — sketch five solutions, no letters/numbers, quantity over quality.
  • Prototype (25 min) — build one rough version of the lesson move on index cards or sticky notes.
  • Test (15 min) — share with a partner using the d.school feedback grid (works / fails / questions / new ideas).
  • Commit (10 min) — pick the day next week the move will ship. Write it on a public list.

Phase II · G

Equity, inclusion, and ML/IEP support

Greenfield Prep’s population is ~28% multilingual and includes a meaningful inclusion population in both Math and Science. Survey responses from inclusion and ML-heavy classrooms named differentiation as the top need. Three design moves embed equity into the program rather than treating it as a separate workstream:

  1. Q3 dedicated focus. The third quarter of every coaching cycle and the Q3 sprint are explicitly about differentiation with existing tools. Every teacher experiences a focused differentiation cycle.
  2. Two lead teachers anchor the work. Mr. Guzman (inclusion Math) and Ms. Mariscal (ML density highest in her room) run peer-coaching slots and pilot the tool combos (text-to-speech, leveled practice, Google Translate + Newsela).
  3. Differentiation indicators in the dashboard. The Looker Studio dashboard reports separately on coaching cycles centered on ML and IEP students, so the work is visible and accountable.

Phase III · C

Pilot & focused-group testing

The capstone window is the pilot. Rather than rolling out to all 18 teachers in August and hoping, four teachers will run a full coaching cycle twice in July, with the explicit goal of breaking the protocol before the school year starts. The pilot is structured as a fast-feedback loop:

  1. Week 1 (Jun 9–13) — Pilot teachers onboarded. Each reviews the Plan/Observe/Reflect/Apply cards and gives written feedback before any cycle runs. The protocol is revised on Day 5 based on that feedback.
  2. Week 2–3 — Pilot teachers complete Cycle 1, focused on a routine of their choice. Coach observes; written reflection captured.
  3. Week 4 (Jun 30) — Pilot retrospective #1: what worked, what failed, what would they change. Cycle protocol revised again.
  4. Weeks 5–7 — Cycle 2 with the revised protocol. Coaching dashboard goes live during this window; pilot teachers test that as well.
  5. Week 8 (Jul 28–31) — Pilot retrospective #2. Final protocol locked. Schoolwide launch plan published.

Why a focused-group pilot matters

  • It surfaces structural problems that a written plan cannot. (E.g., the “observe” step is the most likely to break because of scheduling conflicts with planning periods.)
  • It produces internal champions. The four pilot teachers, by August, are co-owners of the protocol and become the most credible advocates with their colleagues.
  • It de-risks the rollout. If something is going to fail, it should fail in July with four teachers in the building, not in October with eighteen.
  • It generates the schoolwide artifacts (cycle templates, dashboard screenshots, a video walkthrough) that the August onboarding will use.

Total program cost (excluding existing FTE): approximately $9,500 for the year. The dominant cost is people-time, which is already substantially in place. The program is unusually inexpensive for the scope because almost every tool is already licensed.

What we are not buying

  • No new LMS. Google Classroom is sufficient.
  • No new device hardware. The Chromebook fleet is the right asset; the gap is in usage, not in count.
  • No proprietary “coaching platform” software. Google Drive + Looker Studio replaces it at zero cost.
  • No external consultant as primary coach. The credibility cost would outweigh the expertise gain.

Cohort review, references, and source artifacts

Cohort feedback

“Three layers is the right number. Don’t collapse the sprint into the PLC — people will lose the playful frame and it will become work.”  — cohort member, M42 small group
“Make sure the differentiation strand isn’t buried at Q3. ML/IEP needs to show up in cycle 1 as a sub-bullet, not as a quarter-three theme.”  — cohort member, M42 small group

Both pieces of feedback are reflected in the document above — the sprint is preserved as its own layer (Phase II.D), and a differentiation indicator is built into the dashboard (Phase II.E) and into every quarter’s coaching focus, not only Q3.
